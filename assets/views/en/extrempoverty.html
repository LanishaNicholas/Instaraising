Extreme Poverty: A Real Challenge for Policymakers

Nearly 700 million individuals globally are currently living in extreme poverty, surviving on less than $2.15 a day. Over half of these individuals are in Sub-Saharan Africa.

After decades of declining global poverty, crises and shocks from 2020 to 2022 halted progress, causing a three-year regression. Particularly hit were low-income countries, which experienced increased poverty during this period and have yet to recover or bridge the poverty gap.

Midway through the Sustainable Development Goals timeline, the world is not on track to eradicate extreme poverty by 2030. Predictions suggest that around 600 million people will still face extreme poverty by that year.

Regions where extreme poverty is most entrenched include parts of Sub-Saharan Africa, conflict zones, and rural areas. Additionally, nearly half of the global population lives on less than $6.85 a day, a threshold for upper-middle-income countries. Children, who make up 31% of the population, are disproportionately affected, constituting over half of those in extreme poverty. They are more than twice as likely to be in this situation compared to adults.

Food insecurity is a known catalyst for social unrest. The cessation of WFP aid might lead to increased tensions both within communities and towards refugee populations. It could potentially trigger a rise in crime rates, as desperate individuals resort to extreme measures to feed themselves and their families. Furthermore, it could strain already tenuous relations with neighboring countries, as people might cross borders in search of food and shelter.

Addressing poverty comprehensively is essential. Nations must not only focus on income disparities but also improve access to health, education, and basic infrastructure to tackle inequality effectively. Inequality spiked globally in 2020 for the first time in several years, with the pandemic disproportionately impacting the poorest. Their income losses were double those of the wealthiest, and they faced significant setbacks in health and education. Without policy intervention, these challenges could have enduring effects on their future earning potential.

Addressing income, education, and opportunity inequalities holistically is crucial. Reducing disparities can enhance social cohesion and overall well-being. Policymakers need to prioritize economic growth while safeguarding the vulnerable. Employment is key to combating poverty and inequality. Focusing on empowering women, girls, and youth can have a cascading positive effect on communities and future generations.
